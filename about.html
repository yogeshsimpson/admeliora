About Erin

I followed what I actually loved: the people, not the board.

A coach who grew up on Montana ground, thinks in systems, and believes the lasting kind of change is slow.

The short version

About Erin

I started as an agile coach. I loved the work itself — breaking work down, moving it to done, picking up the next important piece. There's a clean satisfaction in that, and it still serves me.

But somewhere in those rooms with teams, I noticed what I actually loved was the people. Not the board, the people standing in front of it. So I followed that, first through co-active coaching, then ORSC (Organizational, Relationship & Systems Coaching). That's when the work started to land differently — bigger, slower, more lasting. Now I bring in somatic awareness and Clean Language alongside the agile bones that never left me. I still think in terms of ready and done. I still believe in transparency. I just hold a wider view of what's actually moving.

I work with agile coaches, organizational leaders, and teams navigating the messy middle of real change.

I'm not finished learning how to do this well. I don't expect to be.

How the work actually moves

Real change is long. It's a nudge from alongside, not a shove from in front.

Most people think systemic change is a switch: stop doing one thing, start doing another. Buy the framework, install it, done.

It isn't that, in my experience.

I once watched one of my dogs, a young Maremma, come up next to our old lab — arthritic, half-blind, struggling in the dark. The Maremma didn't bump her or rush her. She just stayed close, steady, and walked the old girl back toward the door at her own pace.

That's closer to what this work actually looks like. Yes, I teach new skills — feedback, alignment, navigating change. But even that is a nudge, not an about-face. Systemic change asks for more patience than people plan for: the system patient with itself, leadership patient with the system, me patient with whatever is actually happening in the room.

Slow change is the kind that holds.

Ad meliora — toward better things. After all these years, that's still exactly what it means. Not arrival. Not a finish line. Just the walking, and the belief that tomorrow can be better than today — and the day after that, and the day after that.
